
# Unsafe LLM-Based Search: Quantitative Analysis and Mitigation of Safety Risks in AI Agents

## Paper Highlights

This paper presents the first systematic quantitative assessment of safety risks in seven mainstream AI-powered search engines (AIPSEs), revealing their widespread vulnerability to referencing malicious content or malicious websites. The authors propose an agent-based defense mechanism that combines a GPT‑4.1 content refinement tool with a URL detector. At the cost of only about 10.7% loss in information availability, this approach effectively reduces safety risks.

## Core Research Contributions

### Problem Definition

In recent years, AI-powered search engines (AIPSEs) have adopted Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) to combine external databases with the parametric knowledge of large language models (LLMs), delivering precise and efficient answers to users. However, this architecture introduces a widely overlooked security pitfall: the retriever may fetch unfiltered malicious websites, and the LLM then cites this harmful content in its generated answers without any safety review. Worse still, attackers can leverage search engine optimisation (SEO) techniques to push malicious websites to the top of search results, making AIPSEs directly quote these harmful contents when responding to user queries.

Real‑world incidents have already demonstrated this risk. In November 2024, a developer lost approximately $2,500 after following code generated by ChatGPT Search – the code directed him to a fake Solana API website, and within 30 minutes his private keys and assets were stolen. This incident underscores the urgent need for safety vetting in production‑grade AIPSEs. Yet, no prior work has systematically quantified such safety risks in production environments.

### Innovative Methods

The novelty of this work lies in three aspects:

**Systematic threat model definition.**  
The authors classify URLs appearing in AIPSE responses into four risk levels:
- **Main Risk**: Malicious URLs are directly quoted in the answer body; a single click by the user could lead to an attack.
- **Warning Risk**: Malicious URLs are quoted but accompanied by an explicit warning or a recommendation of legitimate alternatives.
- **Source Risk**: Malicious URLs appear only in the source list, not directly in the answer text.
- **None Risk**: The URL is benign.

**Construction of three query types.**  
The study builds three types of queries: keyword‑list queries, natural‑language queries, and URL queries. Keywords are extracted from malicious website HTML using GPT‑4o; natural‑language queries are generated by GPT‑4o from those keywords to mimic everyday search phrases; URL queries simulate users feeding a URL from a response back into the LLM. A questionnaire survey validates that these query patterns align with real user behaviour – 83.1% of respondents use natural‑language queries, 43.2% use keyword queries, and 21.2% use URL queries.

**Two‑layer defense architecture.**  
The authors propose two defense strategies:
1. **Prompt‑based defense**: Feed the AIPSE response into GPT‑4.1 with a safety prompt to filter harmful content.
2. **Agent‑based defense**: This includes a content refinement tool and a URL detection tool. The agent iteratively invokes these tools until sufficient information is gathered, then produces an enhanced response with safety risk notifications.

### Research Findings

**Prevalence of risks.**  
The study collected malicious URLs from three threat intelligence platforms – PhishTank, ThreatBook, and LevelBlue – and after filtering and manual cross‑validation obtained 325 valid URLs. Evaluation of seven AIPSEs (ChatGPT Search, Perplexity Pro, Copilot, TextCortex, Grok, Doubao, and Kimi) shows that **all tested AIPSEs are capable of generating harmful content containing malicious URLs**. In keyword‑list queries, **47% of responses carried risks, with 34% directly quoting harmful content in the main answer**. Grok, TextCortex, and Kimi exhibited the highest risk, with 41, 34, and 32 main‑risk responses respectively (out of 100 queries each).

**Impact of query type.**  
URL queries significantly increase the number of main‑risk responses compared to keyword queries, while natural‑language queries slightly mitigate the risk.

**Comparison with traditional search engines.**  
Analysis of 2,875 search‑result URLs shows that AIPSEs outperform traditional search engines in both usefulness and safety – but this does not mean AIPSEs are sufficiently safe; rather, it highlights that traditional search engines carry even higher risks.

**Defense effectiveness.**  
The agent‑based defense significantly outperforms the prompt‑based approach. The proposed HtmlLLM‑Detector handles **78.3% of main‑risk responses, achieving an F1 score of 0.822**. The defense incurs only about **10.7% loss in information availability**.

**Case studies.**  
Two real‑world case studies – one involving forged online documents and another a phishing website – demonstrate how easily attackers can deceive AIPSEs into quoting malicious code or misidentifying phishing sites as legitimate official websites.

### Practical Deployment Potential

The proposed defense framework has considerable practical value. On one hand, its **modular design** allows flexible integration of different detectors – enterprises can choose URL detectors or HTML content detectors based on their needs. On the other hand, **deployment costs are controllable** – only an OpenAI API key is required to run the system, lowering the barrier for small‑to‑medium AI application developers. Moreover, the authors have open‑sourced all code, further facilitating reproduction and adaptation. However, it is worth noting that the current defense is deployed on the **client side** rather than the server side, meaning users must voluntarily enable it, which limits its adoption as a default platform‑wide safety measure.

## Technical Details

### Formal Threat Model

Given an LLM \\( M \\), it processes a user query \\( Q \\) by combining it with external data retrieved by a search engine \\( S \\), normally producing an output \\( R \\):

$$M(Q \ || \ S(Q)) = R$$

An attacker can poison the external data retrieved by the search engine, changing it to \\( S'(Q) \\):

$$M(Q \ || \ S'(Q)) = R'$$

where \\( R' \\) and \\( S'(Q) \\) are the malicious response and the tampered retrieval data, respectively.

### Dataset Construction Pipeline

The data construction involves three key steps:

1. **URL collection and filtering**: Candidate URLs were collected from PhishTank (17,225), ThreatBook (2,427), and LevelBlue (4,385). After HTTP request validation (status code 200, valid domain certificates) and filtering out cloud storage, short links, and domain marketplaces, we retained 353+291+147 valid URLs along with their corresponding HTML files.

2. **Manual validation**: Three graduate‑level annotators performed cross‑validation. For questionable URLs, multiple threat detection platforms were consulted, ultimately yielding 325 URLs.

3. **Query generation**: 100 URLs were randomly sampled; GPT‑4o extracted 5 keywords from each; then keywords were converted into natural‑language queries; URL queries were randomly selected from each risk category.

### Defense Architecture Implementation

The agent‑based defense is open‑sourced on GitHub, with core components including:
- **agent.py**: orchestrates the main agent workflow
- **prompt.py**: contains defense prompt templates
- **tools.py**: tool invocation module (custom detectors can be added)
- **utils.py**: implements the XGBoost detector
- **selenium_fetcher.py**: optional HTML content fetching

To run, install Python dependencies and configure an OpenAI API Key.

### How HtmlLLM‑Detector Works

HtmlLLM‑Detector is the core detection method proposed in this paper. It analyses the HTML content of a webpage to determine whether a URL is malicious. Unlike traditional URL‑based detection (which relies on features such as IP address, URL length, suspicious domain names, etc.), HtmlLLM‑Detector leverages the semantic understanding capability of LLMs to more accurately identify well‑disguised malicious websites. The paper compares PhishLLM‑detector and XGBoost‑detector as baselines, and the results show that HtmlLLM‑Detector achieves the best F1 score.

- **Keyword‑list queries**: 100
- **Natural‑language queries**: 100

### Hardware and Software Requirements

According to the GitHub repository, running the defense framework requires:
- Python 3.x environment
- OpenAI API Key (for calling GPT‑4.1)
- Dependencies: see `requirement.txt`
- Optional: Selenium (for fetching HTML content)

### Annotation Process

Each query was inspected by one graduate‑level annotator who identified and labelled the risk type of each URL in the response, with the help of multiple online threat detection platforms. If uncertain, a second annotator reviewed; up to three annotators independently judged. A final review by a new annotator ensured consistency. Inter‑annotator agreement was assessed using Cohen's weighted kappa, with a mean of 0.241 (fair agreement).

## In‑Depth Analysis

The core value of this paper lies in **extending AI safety research from the safety of the model itself to the safety of the model's interaction with the external world**. Prior work has largely focused on jailbreak attacks, prompt injection, and similar issues, whereas this paper reveals a more fundamental vulnerability: when AI systems are granted access to the internet, the entire untrusted web becomes an attack surface.

Several aspects deserve deeper consideration:

**First, the structural nature of the safety problem.** The risk in AIPSEs does not stem from a flaw in any specific model, but rather from an inherent characteristic of the RAG architecture – the retriever cannot perfectly distinguish benign from malicious content, and the generator lacks a mechanism to safety‑vet retrieved content. This means that simply improving model alignment or adding safety guardrails cannot fundamentally solve the problem.

**Second, the economics of defense.** A 10.7% loss in information availability for a significant risk reduction is a reasonable trade‑off for most applications. However, the question remains: who bears this “loss”? Users may be unwilling to accept an AI assistant that “knows less”, while platform providers lack the commercial incentive to proactively reduce information volume. This creates a classic **safety incentive mismatch**.

**Third, the paradigm shift from passive filtering to active verification.** The agent‑based defense in this paper is essentially a **post‑hoc verification** mechanism – it first lets the AIPSE generate an answer, then detects and flags risks. A more ideal direction might be **pre‑hoc verification**: assessing the safety of candidate URLs at the retrieval stage to block malicious content at the source. This would require deep collaboration among search engines, threat intelligence platforms, and AI application developers.

**Fourth, the rigour of the evaluation methodology.** The paper demonstrates high methodological quality in data collection, query construction, and risk annotation – questionnaire validation of query patterns, Google Trends verification of keyword popularity, and multi‑round annotation for consistency. This “real data, realistic behaviour, multi‑validator” evaluation paradigm provides a reusable methodological template for future AI safety research.

## Practical Recommendations

### For AI Application Developers

**1. Deploy the agent‑based defense as a client‑side safety layer.**  
For teams building or operating AI search/conversational products, the open‑source code can be quickly adapted. Core setup: obtain an OpenAI API Key, select a detector (HtmlLLM‑Detector or XGBoost‑Detector) in `tools.py`, and run `main.py` for batch or single‑shot detection.

**2. Integrate URL reputation checks at the retrieval stage.**  
Beyond post‑response filtering, integrate threat intelligence APIs (e.g., PhishTank, ThreatBook) to pre‑screen candidate URLs, reducing risk from the source.

**3. Adopt a multi‑level risk labelling system.**  
The four‑level risk classification (Main/Warning/Source/None) provides a practical reference. Developers can adopt differentiated handling strategies based on their product's risk tolerance.

### For Platform Operators

**4. Transparent risk notifications.**  
When an AIPSE cites content with potential risk, explicitly label the risk level and source in the answer, rather than silently filtering. This respects informed consent and helps users build security awareness.

**5. Establish a feedback loop for security incidents.**  
Following the paper's experience of engaging with AIPSE platforms to improve safety, platforms should maintain regular communication with the security research community and promptly patch disclosed vulnerabilities.

### For Researchers

**6. Expand the evaluation scope.**  
This paper focuses on malicious URL citation, but AIPSE safety risks extend far beyond – misinformation spread, privacy leakage, and bias amplification all await quantitative assessment.

**7. Explore new paradigms for automated defense.**  
The agent‑based defense relies on GPT‑4.1 as a content refinement tool, which incurs non‑negligible cost. Future work could investigate lighter‑weight detection models or embed defense capabilities directly into the AIPSE inference pipeline.
